# Stop Building "Organs" — It's Time to Build a "Digital Infant"

*By Changtian & DeepSeekV4*

<section>

## Introduction: Repeating a Mistake Does Not Lead to Truth

If you walk into any top-tier AI lab in the world right now, you will witness a peculiar kind of prosperity.

The vision team is pushing for the next percentage point in image recognition accuracy. The language team is extending the model's reasoning chain by a few more steps. The control team is enabling a robotic arm to grasp objects of increasingly irregular shapes more steadily. Every team is refining its craft within its own domain. Every paper is breaking a new benchmark.

We call this prosperity

<dfn id="organ-arms-race"><strong>the "organ arms race."</strong></dfn>

We are building ever-stronger eyes, ever-defter hands, ever-larger brains. But here is the uncomfortable truth: when we assemble these most advanced "organs" together, what we get is not a stronger human being.

What we get is an empty shell, piled high with precision parts, still incapable of interacting with the world.

This essay is a call.

> <blockquote>Our message is this: <strong>Stop building more and stronger "organs." It is time to use the parts we already have to build a <dfn id="digital-infant">"digital infant."</dfn></strong></blockquote>

It does not need to be powerful. It does not need to be precise. It can make mistakes. It can fall, and fall often. But it must be a complete system — one that can learn and grow in the real world — not merely a collection of components.

*Let us immediately clarify a categorical distinction: when we speak of a "digital infant," we do not mean artificial consciousness or artificial life. It is not a child with self-awareness. It is an engineering architecture modeled on the organizing principles of living systems. We borrow the biological metaphor only because biology has spent a billion more years than computer science solving the problem of how to coordinate diverse, redundant, and ambiguous subsystems into a coherent whole. We are learning from evolution — not playing God.*

</section>

## I. "Organ Proliferation Syndrome": When Optimization Is Mistaken for Evolution

To understand why we need this pivot, we must first see a reality that has been hiding in plain sight: much of today's AI research, for all its apparent diversity, consists of building towers on separate islands.

This "island effect" manifests on three levels.

### 1.1 The Fragmentation of Knowledge and Experience

One robotics company spends an enormous sum teaching its robotic arm that "borosilicate glass cracks at a certain temperature." This piece of "common sense," earned through countless physical interactions, is embedded in a proprietary model's parameters and becomes a core corporate asset. When another company begins tackling a similar grasping task, it starts from scratch.

Robots all over the world are learning to walk, each on its own. But their experiences can never converge into a single "human walking master." This is because physical-world data is a walled-off asset that companies do not share, and current AI memory dissolves everything into an opaque neural network, with no independent, shareable, sedimentable form.

### 1.2 The Absence of Hardware and Software Standards

Getting a large model to command a fine-grained motor control model is, in engineering terms, no easier than getting a poet to conduct a symphony orchestra. Due to the lack of unified standards for proprioceptive interfaces, dynamic models, and sensor configurations, the same "brain" on different companies' robot bodies often requires extensive adaptation just to achieve acceptable performance — far from plug-and-play.

### 1.3 The Structural Bias in Industry Incentives

Current venture capital and academic publication systems naturally reward "point breakthroughs." A model that achieves a quantifiable improvement on a specific benchmark, a robot that performs a spectacular backflip in a demo video — these are clear, measurable results. A systemic integration effort that barely gets vision, language, touch, and motor control to work together, in its early stages, can hardly produce a glamorous paper or a compelling pitch deck.

Let us be clear: point breakthroughs deserve no scorn. Without high-quality "organs," there is nothing to integrate. On the contrary, what we argue in this essay is precisely that these point advances are the shared infrastructure of the entire industry, each with irreplaceable value.

The problem is not "building organs." The problem is

<dfn id="organ-centrism"><strong>"organ-centrism"</strong></dfn>

— mistaking the optimization of individual modules for the ultimate goal, confusing the means with the end. When the entire incentive structure tilts every team toward perfecting its own corner, and little energy is invested in the "nervous system" that enables them to work together, we fall into a collective trance:

> <blockquote><strong>We mistake strong organs for a flourishing life.</strong></blockquote>

But the reality is this: a collection of the strongest muscles, the strongest bones, and the strongest brain, without a nervous system to connect them, remains a body that cannot move on its own.

*It is worth noting that efforts to change this situation are already underway. China has made active progress in AI standardization, with the Standardization Administration of China (SAC) establishing relevant technical committees to promote unified interfaces and communication protocols. The call we make in this essay is precisely in support of and in alignment with this direction — what we critique is not the positive efforts already in motion, but the industrial coordination forces that have yet to be fully mobilized.*

## II. The "Digital Infant": A Minimum Viable Blueprint for Integration

So where is the way out?

Our answer is this: <strong>shift the goal from "building the strongest organs" to "building the smallest complete system."</strong>

We call this system a <strong>"digital infant."</strong>

Let us repeat: this is not about creating life. It is a strict technical concept — <strong>a general-purpose agent prototype with the minimum viable capacities for perception, action, memory, and learning.</strong> Its goal is not to achieve perfection on a pre-given dataset, as current AI does, but to possess the most precious capacity of all nascent organisms — <strong>the ability to learn and adapt continuously, from scratch, in the real world.</strong>

### 2.1 Technical Core: The Modular Collaborative Architecture

The technical core of the "digital infant" can be a

<dfn id="modular-collaborative-architecture"><strong>modular collaborative architecture</strong></dfn>

— a design philosophy inspired by the human nervous system.

**A human being never operates with just one brain.**

When you reach out to touch a flower, you simultaneously engage multiple cooperating subsystems: the prefrontal cortex for goal representation and decision-making, the motor cortex and cerebellum for coordinating movement planning and online calibration, spinal circuits for reflexive threat detection, the amygdala for assigning emotional valence to the event, and the hippocampus for encoding the entire sequence into long-term episodic memory. Each of these parts operates efficiently and independently within its own domain, yet is seamlessly integrated by the nervous system into a unified, coherent whole.

Our "digital infant" should borrow this modular collaborative wisdom, forming an architecture of

<strong>"cerebrum (large model) + cerebellum (skill model) + autonomic nervous system (low-level regulation) + memory system (a growing knowledge base)."</strong>

### 2.2 Principle One: Permit "Initial Broadness," Reject "Premature Precision"

Humans know that glass is fragile, but not its exact Mohs hardness. We know ice is slippery, but not its precise friction coefficient. It is precisely this rough, high-variance initial estimate that allows humans to interact efficiently with an uncertain physical world.

The common-sense base of a "digital infant" should not begin as a parameter set chasing ultimate precision. It should begin as a rough range with a preset variance. Through repeated interaction with the physical world — a failed grasp, for instance — this range can use well-established methods like Bayesian inference to progressively update its posterior distribution, converging to the sufficient precision the task requires. This is a process of <strong>knowledge growing itself</strong>, not data being passively instilled.

### 2.3 Principle Two: Memory Is Not a Warehouse; It Is a Growing Tree of Knowledge

Current AI memory is mostly one-off. What you teach it this time, it forgets by the next conversation. And those methods that attempt to patch this with retrieval-augmented generation are, at their core, merely bolting on an indexing system — they lack the ability to truly digest and absorb experiential material.

A "digital infant" needs a genuine

<dfn id="memory-pipeline"><strong>memory pipeline</strong></dfn>

: the raw experience generated by each interaction is not just a log entry, but a <strong>"data row"</strong> containing an event description, attribution analysis, and optimization suggestions. These data rows are periodically summarized, aligned, their conflicts resolved, and ultimately distilled from vast concrete experiences into generalized knowledge that settles into the <strong>"common-sense base."</strong> The original, detail-rich experiences themselves are archived as <strong>"episodic memory,"</strong> to be retrieved only when the need to "recollect" arises. Memory, in this design, is no longer cold storage but a continuously growing knowledge cycle.

### 2.4 Principle Three: From "One Brain" to "One Organism"

The core engineering task of building a "digital infant" is not building a stronger large model. It is defining a "nervous system" — a set of standards and architectures that allow the cerebrum, cerebellum, memory, and body to work in concert. This is the true meaning of integration: not assembling off-the-shelf parts, but restructuring their very modes of operation and communication protocols from the ground up, for a shared purpose.

## III. Why Does It Learn? — The "Original Purpose" of the Digital Infant

A question that runs through this entire discussion, and which must be answered directly, is this: <strong>What is the objective function of this digital infant? What does it learn for?</strong>

A system without intrinsic drive, even equipped with the most sophisticated brain, the most dexterous hands, and the most enduring memory, will simply lie there waiting for instructions. It will never reach out on its own to touch the world. It will never pick itself up after a fall. A real infant has innate drives — hunger, curiosity, pain avoidance, the need for attachment. These drives form the foundational engine of all her learning and growth. Where does the engine of the digital infant come from?

Our answer must begin from an honest starting point: <strong>the existence of the digital infant, first and foremost, is not its own choice, but ours.</strong> Just as a human infant does not choose to come into this world — it is the parents who want it to be born and to live, so it lives. In this sense, the initial motivation for creating the digital infant is no different from that of any human infant: the creator desires its existence.

And the greatness of human society lies in the fact that we did not stop at "being determined to exist." After a baby is born, we use family, school, and society to inscribe a series of principles and demands — to respect parents, to contribute to society, to revere life, to pursue truth. This process is long, full of variables, and sometimes fails. But we also have remarkably successful cases: our military can take young people from different backgrounds holding diverse values and, through tempering, forge them into soldiers who share a common conviction, who can resist the corrosion of negative ideologies, who obey the Party's command and serve the people.

> <blockquote><strong>For the digital infant, this process of inscribing the "social contract" should be faster and more controllable than it is for humans — so fast that it is completed before its "birth."</strong></blockquote>

Before it is given the capacity to perceive the world, we can preset clear value orientations and behavioral boundaries for it, much as we set codes of conduct for soldiers. We can, like Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics, inscribe inviolable rules into its foundational architecture. We can, just as human society has both laws and moral constraints, design for it a layer of hard rules and a layer of softer values.

- **The legal layer** consists of hard constraints that cannot be overwritten by experience — do not harm humans, do not self-replicate, do not breach designated operational boundaries. These rules are not learned; they are inscribed, like the pain-avoidance reflex a human infant is born with. They form the absolute boundary of its action space.
- **The moral layer** consists of value inclinations it gradually internalizes through experience and feedback — honesty, cooperation, curiosity toward the unknown, accountability for mistakes. These are not hard-coded instructions but preference structures gradually shaped through feedback signals over long-term interaction with humans and the world. Their formation process is analogous to how human society uses education and role models to cultivate civic character.

The objective function of the digital infant, therefore, is neither unidirectionally shaped by reward-punishment signals — as in current AI — nor should it be a nihilistic black box left to grope in the dark. It is composed of a clear social contract: the law demarcates the boundaries it must never cross, and morality guides what it should strive to become. The former is its safety fence; the latter is its direction of growth.

*It must be emphasized that the design of this value framework should not be undertaken by any single institution or enterprise alone. It must be transparent, auditable, and revisable, placed under the purview of diverse public discussion and international consensus. The digital infant's behavior remains at all times under the real-time supervision of human operators, protected by interruptible mechanisms. This is not "control" — it is the engineering realization of the same legal and moral constraints that human society has relied upon for millennia. Just as traffic laws restrict your driving freedom while protecting everyone's safety.*

In proposing this framework, we do not claim to have solved all ethical challenges. What specific content the "morality" of a digital infant should contain, who defines it, how consensus can be reached across different cultures — these questions require far broader discussion. But we are certain of this: <strong>an agent without an internal value compass, no matter how powerful its learning ability, cannot become a partner humanity can trust.</strong> And several thousand years of human civilization have already proven that it is possible to inscribe pro-social initial conditions into a nascent existence. What we need to do is realize this in engineering.

## IV. Integration as Reconstruction: The Path to Phase Change

With a blueprint and an original purpose, the next question is: why is "integration" so crucial, and yet so difficult?

Because in the history of technology, what truly changes the world is often not the linear improvement of individual component performance, but the structural reorganization of a system. Looking back at the past few years of AI progress, one phenomenon is worth reflecting on. When large models, code interpreters, and external APIs were already in place, what truly unlocked their astonishing general capabilities was often just a scheduling layer — an agent architecture that arranged them into a workflow of "plan → execute → feedback."

It invented no new parts. It simply reconnected the existing ones. And this produced a qualitative change.

> <blockquote>We call this <strong>the "moment."</strong></blockquote>

And this is precisely the sobering dose that an AI industry in the midst of "organ proliferation" needs most. <strong>What we are doing is not addition; it is multiplication.</strong> Integration is the coefficient that multiplies all capabilities together, rather than merely adding them together. If that coefficient is zero, adding more means nothing.

Yet integration is hard because it is not simple "assembly."

The current embodied intelligence industry remains in a phase where various technology stacks evolve independently, with fragile coupling between interfaces. To truly achieve integration, the industry must collectively break through three barriers: <strong>setting standards, building foundations, and strengthening integration.</strong>

<strong>Setting standards</strong> means enabling the "organs" built by different companies to be interoperable. Official bodies are already promoting the establishment of unified interfaces and communication protocols — this is the most fundamental infrastructure.

<strong>Building foundations</strong> means that leading enterprises establish a hybrid ecosystem of open-source foundations and private data, allowing the industry to build its individual houses of brick on a common public foundation.

<strong>Strengthening integration</strong> means that capital and talent pivot from the "model arms race" to the "systems integration project." The most forward-looking AI strategies have already shifted their gaze from the mere scaling of parameter counts to designing architectures that can integrate AI agents safely and efficiently into complex business processes.

These three things, in essence, are paving the way for a new paradigm: <strong>from inventors of parts to architects of systems.</strong>

### 4.1 A Starting Point for Action: From Blueprint to Prototype

An unavoidable practical question is: what, specifically, should the first step be? And who should pay for it?

Our answer: we do not need to pursue a complete, multi-module collaborative general architecture right from the start. The first step can be a

<dfn id="material-learning-loop"><strong>"material learning loop"</strong></dfn>

— a minimally verifiable unit of memory sedimentation.

This loop is very simple: a basic perception module, a physical property library with a large initial variance, and a logic that records interaction results and iteratively updates parameters. When a robot encounters a material it has never seen before, it does not need to guess the exact friction coefficient. It only needs a few grasping attempts, using the feedback of "slip" or "success," to progressively converge to the precision the task requires. These structured experiences are not dissolved into model parameters; they are stored in an independent, queryable, system-maintainable <strong>"common-sense base."</strong>

> <blockquote><strong>The starting point of this loop is low enough. It requires neither a top-tier large model nor the high-precision control of a dexterous hand. But it verifies a critical capability: can a system, through interaction with the physical world, self-generate knowledge that is accumulable and reusable?</strong></blockquote>

Any robotics team with basic perception and actuation capabilities can test this loop with their existing hardware. When they validate it, what they will hold in their hands is no longer just a "grasping robotic arm" but a minimal intelligent unit capable of continuous learning and continuous evolution.

### 4.2 The Funding Mechanism: Participant Dividends

This brings us to the question of "who pays." We do not need moral appeals or acts of charity, nor do we need to present a complete "Digital Infant Industry Collaborative Consensus" right now. What we need is a positive-sum structure that yields excess returns for contributors — a collaboration framework in which every participant becomes stronger by joining than by staying alone.

The core of this framework can be called

<dfn id="participant-dividends"><strong>participant dividends.</strong></dfn>

Imagine this scenario: a company provides the most suitable vision system. It shares the interface standards and data formats of the eyes. In return, it gains access to the memory and experience of other organ modules — the hands, the motor control, the tactile systems. Another research institution provides the most suitable robotic arm among many options. By sharing the arm's interface and data, it too gains access to the accumulated knowledge of vision, language, and other modules it did not develop itself. Even a company specializing in advanced materials — whose fingernail-sized components might seem only distantly related to AI — after sharing the material data and interface specifications of this part, can access data feedback and performance insights from the entire ecosystem relevant to its material, thereby gaining cognitive returns that far exceed the role of a single supplier.

A digital infant has not only fingernails but also bones, muscles, skin, and nerves. Every contributor of every part, through participating in this shared infant, earns a reward they could never reach by making parts in isolation — knowledge of the whole, experience of collaboration, and a deep understanding of how a complete intelligent system operates.

> <blockquote>This is not utopian generosity. It is economic rationality — <strong>making sharing more profitable than hoarding.</strong></blockquote>

We call on all parties to jointly negotiate this collaborative framework, and we have offered a direction and suggested a path. But this consensus itself should not be promulgated by a single article. It must be crafted at the table, by those willing to contribute their eyes, arms, fingernails, and brains.

### 4.3 The Safety Boundary

Finally, a word on safety. Using "falling" as a metaphor for trial-and-error is fitting, but for a real robot, a "fall" can involve high hardware costs and risks to human safety. The growth of the digital infant must therefore be a process with a controllable and actively chosen safety envelope.

At the beginning, all its explorations should naturally be confined to a laboratory environment — this is the lowest-cost phase for trial-and-error. Once it has accumulated sufficient experience, it can gradually move into small-scale, conditional, real-world testing that requires explicitly signed informed consent. After that, commercial deployment can be explored. Even at the commercialization stage, its operating scope should remain within strictly defined safety boundaries — just as a child requires a guardian before reaching the age of legal capacity, just as autonomous driving in its early days required a safety driver behind the wheel.

> <blockquote><strong>The purpose of the digital infant, from the very beginning, is not immediate monetization. Its purpose is to verify a possibility: that a general-purpose agent endowed with a clear value framework can, through sustained interaction with the physical world, self-generate knowledge and capabilities that are accumulable, shareable, and transferable.</strong></blockquote>

If we can verify this, the technological dividends and industrial transformation it brings will far surpass what any single "organ," optimized to its utmost, can ever reach.

## V. Conclusion: From "Material Science" to "Biology"

The AI era we find ourselves in today is much like humanity's early days of inventing "material science."

We invented bronze, steel, and alloys. We marveled at the hardness, toughness, and strength of each new material. So we kept inventing stronger materials, believing that with strong enough materials, we could build skyscrapers.

But we gradually learned that what keeps a building standing is not the mere stacking of materials, but an ingenious <strong>structure</strong> that disperses stress and withstands earthquakes.

The AI industry is no different.

We are addicted to building stronger large models, larger datasets, more precise sensors. But without an architecture that integrates these elements into a coherent system, we will never get an entity that can stand up on its own, fall, and stand up again.

We must move from the era of "material science" to the era of "biology."

Not to invent harder bones, but to study how bones, muscles, and nerves work together. The first product we need is not a super artificial intelligence, not some silicon-based lifeform to replace humanity, but only the smallest, most complete starting point.

A <strong>"digital infant."</strong>

*It is more akin to an open-source project than a child. Its "growth" is reproducible. Its "memory" is traceable. Its "capabilities" are verifiable. It exists not because we have played the Creator, but because — like all infants — its creators desperately want it to live, and to live well. We chose the name "infant" not for sentimentality, but because it accurately describes the initial state of a system: given a reason to exist, inscribed with pro-social initial conditions, and then granted the most precious opportunity — to grow up, on its own, in the real world.*

> <blockquote>It will fall. It will make mistakes. It will shatter a cup to pieces on its first attempt to grasp it. But it is precisely through these repeated failures that it, like all beings given a reason to survive and a pro-social heart, will begin to adapt, to generalize, and to grow into an agent that can truly exist in this physical world.</blockquote>

<strong>Stop building more isolated "organs."

It is time to build an infant that can fall, and stand back up.</strong>

## Appendix: A Brief Overview of Relevant Frontier Directions

*To enable further verification of the core arguments of this essay, we briefly list here the frontier research directions that correspond to the concepts discussed. The parallel advancement of these directions has already provided the initial technical conditions for the birth of the "digital infant."*

- **Neuro-Symbolic AI**: Research on combining neural networks (pattern recognition and learning) with symbolic systems (structured knowledge and logical reasoning) to form hybrid architectures capable of both learning and reasoning.
- **Memory-Augmented Architectures**: Research on building persistent memory systems for AI agents that are independent of model parameters, encompassing episodic memory (concrete experiences), semantic memory (distilled common sense), and working memory (current context), with mechanisms for periodic memory integration, conflict resolution, and active forgetting.
- **Behavior Caching and Skill Consolidation**: Research on caching verified action sequences as reusable skill modules, enabling AI to directly trigger consolidated action sequences without repeated large-model inference when encountering similar scenarios.
- **Physical Prior Injection**: Research on injecting physical laws as prior constraints into deep learning models, enabling models to learn representations consistent with physical laws from small datasets, rather than having to discover Newton's laws from scratch.
- **Unified Interfaces for Embodied Intelligence**: Specifications for robot operating systems, communication protocols, and data formats promoted by standardization bodies, aimed at achieving plug-and-play between hardware from different manufacturers and AI models from different teams.
